\begin{abstract}
We study whether a deep neural architecture originally proposed for limit order book (LOB) forecasting, DeepLOB, can be reproduced on the FI-2010 benchmark and then transferred to a materially different market dataset from Optiver. Our project has two linked goals. First, we reproduce the core CNN--Inception--LSTM pipeline on FI-2010 under two training profiles---a \emph{paper-style} optimizer (primary) and an \emph{adaptive} horizon-aware profile (comparison)---and verify that both achieve strong mid-price direction classification across all five event horizons. For short horizons ($k{\leq}30$ events) the paper profile is primary, reaching accuracy~$0.835$ and MCC~$0.608$ at 10 events; for long horizons ($k{\geq}50$ events) the adaptive profile is primary, achieving MCC~$0.694$ at 100 events (vs $0.684$ under the paper profile). We further analyze engineered microstructure factors, show that price/volume derivative features are consistently predictive, and confirm that DeepLOB outperforms shallow manual-factor baselines. Second, we adapt the architecture to Optiver's two-level book representation (DeepLOBLite) and evaluate transfer learning across three held-out stocks (IDs 42, 17, 82) under rolling-quantile label construction (\texttt{rolling3}), where class boundaries are the rolling historical 33rd and 67th percentiles of absolute $k$-step returns over a 20{,}000-sample window. The universal base model achieves mean Cohen's~$\kappa{=}0.062$ across held-out stocks; per-stock fine-tuning lifts this to $\kappa{=}0.084$, primarily by recalibrating the classifier's decision boundaries to each stock's local return distribution. Specific-stock temporal training underperforms both ($\kappa{=}0.002$), confirming that within-stock nonstationarity is a first-order challenge. Together the results show that DeepLOB reproduces reliably on FI-2010 and that rolling-quantile label adaptation with per-stock fine-tuning is the most effective Optiver transfer strategy identified, though the domain gap remains substantial and motivates continued work.
\end{abstract}

\section{Problem Definition}
This project asks two connected technical questions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Can we faithfully reproduce the main empirical behavior of DeepLOB on the FI-2010 benchmark using a modern PyTorch implementation?
    \item If so, how well does the same modeling idea transfer to a different LOB environment, namely Optiver's two-level auction book data, after architectural adaptation and fine-tuning?
\end{enumerate}

These questions are worth studying for two reasons. First, LOB prediction is a canonical sequential learning problem with strong local structure, temporal dependence, and severe class imbalance, making it a natural testbed for course ideas in representation learning and generalization. Second, successful performance on a benchmark does not guarantee transfer to a new market or data schema. Understanding where transfer succeeds and fails is practically important and scientifically useful.

Our final deliverable is therefore not just a reproduction. It is a controlled study of how far the DeepLOB design carries beyond FI-2010, and what breaks when the data distribution, label process, and book structure change.

\section{Prior Work}
DeepLOB, introduced by Zhang, Zohren, and Roberts \cite{zhang2019deeplob}, is a well-known architecture for high-frequency LOB forecasting. Its core idea is to combine local convolutional pattern extraction, an Inception-style multi-scale module, and an LSTM that aggregates short-term temporal structure before classification. The benchmark most closely associated with this line of work is FI-2010, curated by Ntakaris et al.~\cite{ntakaris2018benchmark}, which provides a standardized setup for mid-price movement prediction across several event horizons.

Earlier work on LOB prediction often relied on handcrafted microstructure features and simpler classifiers. Kercheval and Zhang \cite{kercheval2015modelling}, for example, studied machine learning methods on LOB-derived features, highlighting that engineered factors such as imbalance and order-flow statistics can contain predictive information. This matters for our project because part of our contribution is to compare DeepLOB with manual-factor baselines instead of treating the deep model as a black box.

More broadly, financial time-series forecasting is known to be sensitive to nonstationarity, label definition, and market-specific effects. Methods that work on one dataset can degrade sharply under even moderate shifts in trading regime, tick structure, or normalization scheme. That concern motivates our Optiver study. Rather than proposing a novel architecture from scratch, we investigate whether a proven LOB model can generalize to a new setting with only modest adaptation.

\section{Data and Experimental Setup}
\subsection{FI-2010}
FI-2010 contains normalized limit order book data with 40 input features corresponding to 10 bid/ask levels and associated volumes. Following the standard setup, the task is three-class classification of future mid-price movement (up / stationary / down) at five event horizons: 10, 20, 30, 50, and 100 events ahead. In the codebase these appear as horizon indices $k_\text{idx}\in\{0,\dots,4\}$, or equivalently $k\in\{1,\dots,5\}$.

The training pipeline uses a lookback window of 100 events and a batch size of 32. Following the de-facto FI-2010 split, the last day of each month is used as a test day; the remainder provides training and validation. We evaluate all five horizons under two training profiles and report results for both, so that the full picture---including horizon-specific trade-offs---is visible.

\subsection{Optiver}
The Optiver dataset differs substantially from FI-2010. Each event exposes only two order book levels, yielding 8 raw LOB features after reordering:
\[
\{\texttt{ask\_price1}, \texttt{ask\_size1}, \texttt{bid\_price1}, \texttt{bid\_size1},
\texttt{ask\_price2}, \texttt{ask\_size2}, \texttt{bid\_price2}, \texttt{bid\_size2}\}.
\]

Preprocessing applies \emph{causal} rolling normalization so that each sample is standardized only from past observations, avoiding any lookahead leakage. Labels are constructed from future log mid-price returns. We compare two labeling schemes that differ in how return thresholds are defined:

Labels are constructed via \textbf{rolling-quantile thresholding (\texttt{rolling3})}: class boundaries are the rolling historical 33rd and 67th percentiles of absolute $k$-step returns over a 20{,}000-sample window. This ensures approximately uniform marginal class frequencies across time, and the thresholds adapt continuously to the recent return distribution, making the labeling robust to return-scale differences across stocks. All experiments use the 5-event ($k{=}5$) horizon; held-out stocks are 42, 17, and~82.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
Across both datasets we report accuracy, Cohen's $\kappa$, MCC, and class-wise or weighted F1 scores. For Optiver we additionally inspect regime-style proxy profit statistics; these serve as diagnostic secondary measures rather than claims about a deployable trading strategy.

\subsection{Report Scope}
The final report presents the two FI training profiles and the two Optiver label modes described above, evaluated on all five FI horizons and the $k{=}5$ Optiver horizon. Saved artifacts reside under:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \texttt{results/fi\_paper/} --- FI-2010 paper-profile outputs (primary for $k{=}1$--$3$);
    \item \texttt{results/fi\_adaptive/} --- FI-2010 adaptive-profile outputs (primary for $k{=}4$--$5$; comparison at short horizons);
    \item \texttt{results/optiver/classification\_rolling3\_w20000/} --- Optiver rolling-quantile label outputs (stocks 42/17/82, $k{=}5$).
\end{itemize}

\section{Approach}
\subsection{DeepLOB Reproduction on FI-2010: Two Training Profiles}
Our FI-2010 model follows the DeepLOB design closely. The input tensor has shape $(B,1,T,40)$ with $T=100$. The network contains:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item three convolutional blocks that progressively compress local price-volume structure,
    \item an Inception-style multi-branch module that captures patterns at multiple receptive fields,
    \item a one-layer LSTM with hidden size 64,
    \item a dropout layer and final fully connected classifier over three movement classes.
\end{itemize}

The implementation preserves the paper architecture. However, we found that a literal paper optimizer (large learning rate, uniform hyperparameters across horizons) caused majority-class collapse on shorter horizons. We therefore developed two training profiles and ran both to completion:

\paragraph{Paper profile (primary).} All five horizons use the same training recipe: Adam with learning rate $1\times10^{-3}$, batch size 32, validation-accuracy checkpointing, and no horizon-specific tuning beyond early stopping. This closely mirrors the paper's setup and achieves the strongest short-horizon results (accuracy $0.835$ at 10 events). Loss curves and confusion matrices for this profile are saved under \texttt{results/fi\_paper/}.

\paragraph{Adaptive profile (comparison).} Horizon-aware regularization is applied: shorter horizons ($k{=}1,2$) use macro-F1 or MCC as the checkpointing monitor to resist stationary-class collapse, while longer horizons ($k{=}3,4,5$) monitor validation loss with stronger regularization because they overfit earlier. Specific long-horizon settings include learning rate $7\times10^{-4}$, weight decay $3\times10^{-4}$, dropout $0.30$, label smoothing $0.02$, and gradient clipping at 1.0. The adaptive profile narrows the short-horizon accuracy gap and surpasses the paper profile at $k{=}4$ and $k{=}5$ (MCC $0.675$ and $0.694$ vs $0.666$ and $0.684$ for the paper profile). Outputs are saved under \texttt{results/fi\_adaptive/}.

\subsection{Microstructure Analysis on FI-2010}
Beyond classification, the project includes a CPU-side analysis pipeline that:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item runs Newey--West predictability tests on 104 engineered LOB features,
    \item checks rolling-window significance stability,
    \item measures monotonicity and rank IC behavior,
    \item applies Benjamini--Hochberg FDR control,
    \item trains shallow baselines (ridge logistic regression and two-layer MLP) on manual factors,
    \item computes simple strategy-style statistics from model predictions.
\end{itemize}

This extends the study from ``does the network work?'' to ``what signals are present, and how much value does the deep model add?'' The analysis outputs (factor qualification, feature significance plots, baseline comparison) are shared across both FI profiles since they depend only on the raw data and engineered features, not on the trained model.

\subsection{DeepLOBLite for Optiver: Two Label Modes}
To transfer the method to Optiver, we adapt the network to the two-level book. The main architectural change is at the input interface: the model consumes $(B,1,T,8)$ instead of $(B,1,T,40)$, and the third convolutional block uses a $(1,2)$ kernel. The LSTM and classifier dimensions remain unchanged. We refer to this adapted model as \textbf{DeepLOBLite}.

The Optiver pipeline has three stages:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Universal base training:} train on a pool of source stocks and evaluate zero-shot on held-out stocks.
    \item \textbf{Transfer fine-tuning:} fine-tune the universal model on each held-out stock (head and partial deeper layers), evaluated on a held-out temporal split of the same stock.
    \item \textbf{Specific-stock out-of-sample training:} train a model on an early temporal segment of one stock and evaluate on a later segment of the same stock.
\end{enumerate}

Under \texttt{rolling3}, thresholds are purely rank-based, which pushes label frequencies toward balance and makes the classifier robust to absolute return scale shifts across stocks---an important property when transferring to stocks with different liquidity or volatility profiles.

Training configuration: focal loss, dropout 0.30, weight decay $1\times10^{-4}$, gradient clipping at 1.0, macro-F1-based validation monitoring, and an auxiliary log-return regression head to regularize representations.

\section{Our Contribution}
Our contribution has three distinct parts.

\subsection{Contribution 1: A Reproducible DeepLOB Pipeline with Two Validated Training Profiles}
We reimplemented the end-to-end DeepLOB workflow in a standalone training script and ran two complete training regimes: a paper-faithful optimizer and an adaptive horizon-aware regularization scheme. Documenting both is a real contribution because it surface a property that single-profile reproductions miss: uniform hyperparameter settings achieve high accuracy on short horizons (the regime where the paper optimizer is most effective), while adaptive per-horizon monitoring achieves more balanced class agreement at long horizons. Neither profile dominates unconditionally; knowing when each is preferable is itself a practical result.

\subsection{Contribution 2: Extending the FI-2010 Study Beyond Raw Benchmark Accuracy}
Instead of stopping at classification scores, we asked what the dataset itself reveals about microstructure signals. We tested 104 engineered factors, measured statistical significance with Newey--West corrections, screened them with rolling stability and BH-FDR control, and compared the deep model against manual-factor baselines. This produces an interpretable picture of what information is present in FI-2010 and how DeepLOB relates to simpler alternatives.

\subsection{Contribution 3: Controlled Transfer to Optiver with a Label-Mode Comparison}
The main original research component is the Optiver study. We ran the full three-stage transfer pipeline (universal base training, per-stock fine-tuning, specific-stock out-of-sample training) under rolling-quantile label construction (\texttt{rolling3}) and evaluated transfer to three held-out stocks not seen during base training. The key finding---that per-stock fine-tuning improves mean $\kappa$ from $0.062$ to $0.084$ while specific-stock temporal training regresses to $\kappa{=}0.002$---isolates per-stock classifier recalibration as the effective lever and within-stock temporal drift as the main bottleneck. This is a controlled, quantitative claim supported by three independently evaluated held-out stocks.

\subsection{FI-2010 Baselines and Factor Analysis}
The manual-factor baselines underperform the deep model. Under the paper profile, ridge logistic regression reaches accuracy $0.710$ with near-zero $\kappa$ ($0.004$), and a two-layer MLP reaches accuracy $0.716$ with MCC $0.191$. DeepLOB under the paper profile improves accuracy to $0.835$ and MCC to $0.608$ at the comparable short-horizon setting. This large gap---particularly in $\kappa$ and MCC---demonstrates that the deep architecture extracts timing and shape information that cannot be recovered from a fixed engineered feature set.

Out of 104 engineered features tested, 21 survive the qualification pipeline. Most of the strongest signals belong to the ``price \& volume derivatives'' group (set \texttt{u6}). Feature~1 in this group has $t_\text{NW}=11.29$, and feature~3 has $t_\text{NW}=10.84$. Rolling-window analysis confirms that these factors remain significant across a large fraction of windows, indicating that their predictive content is regime-stable rather than driven by a localized spike.

One honest limitation is that the strategy-style pseudo-PnL diagnostics are weak across both profiles. The short horizons show borderline-positive but insignificant Sharpe-like statistics (k=1 Sharpe $0.072$, $p=0.230$); longer horizons turn negative. This is expected: raw classification accuracy on a benchmark does not imply profitable round-trip trading under realistic frictions.

\subsection{Why Rolling-Quantile Labels and Fine-Tuning Work}

Under \texttt{rolling3}, thresholds are the rolling 33rd/67th percentile of absolute $k$-step returns over a 20{,}000-sample window. This means the held-out stock's ``top third'' return magnitude may not exactly match the scale seen during base training, leading to moderate zero-shot agreement ($\kappa{=}0.062$). However, fine-tuning on even a modest amount of stock-specific data re-anchors the classifier to the stock's local return quantiles, recovering $\kappa{=}0.084$. The fine-tuning step is essentially \emph{per-stock threshold calibration} rather than relearning the feature extractor---which is why it is effective with limited data and why accuracy changes less than $\kappa$ (the model redistributes its predictions without dramatically altering feature alignment).

Specific-stock OOS training ($\kappa{=}0.002$) fails despite using stock-specific data because the training and evaluation temporal segments are well-separated in time. Return-quantile levels drift within a stock over medium timescales, so a model calibrated on an early window cannot reliably set boundaries for a later window. This within-stock temporal drift compounds cross-stock distribution shift and is a key practical bottleneck for transfer to live equity LOB settings.

\subsection{LIME Interpretability Analysis (Optiver)}
To gain insight into which input features drive the fine-tuned model's predictions, we apply \textbf{LIME} (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations) to a representative sample from the held-out set. LIME fits a sparse linear surrogate over perturbed versions of a single input window to approximate the local decision boundary of the deep model.

The case examined is stock~82, $k{=}5$, sample index~731, with true label \emph{Stationary}. This stock was selected because it shows the strongest and most consistent fine-tuning benefit ($\kappa$ rising from $0.077$ to $0.094$), making it the most interpretable post-fine-tuning case.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{../results/optiver/classification_rolling3_w20000/figure9_transfer_lime.png}
\caption{LIME feature-importance explanation for a \emph{Stationary} prediction on stock~82 ($k{=}5$, sample~731). Bar lengths indicate the magnitude of each input feature's local contribution to the predicted class; color indicates direction (positive = supports predicted class). Features corresponding to recent ask/bid price levels dominate the explanation, consistent with the microstructure intuition that short-term price position relative to recent quotes is the strongest signal in a 2-level LOB.}
\label{fig:optiver-lime}
\end{figure}

The LIME explanation in Figure~\ref{fig:optiver-lime} reveals that the fine-tuned model relies primarily on \textbf{recent ask and bid price levels} (the top-ranked features) rather than size features. This is consistent with the intuition that in a shallow 2-level book, price position relative to recent quotes is the most informative signal for predicting near-term direction. Size features contribute smaller and noisier local weights, suggesting the model has learned to discount volume information that is highly variable at the 2-level depth.

This interpretability result connects back to the FI-2010 factor analysis: both datasets show price-derived features as the dominant predictive channel. The key difference is that in FI-2010 the signal is spread across 10 levels and captured via price-derivative engineered factors, while in Optiver DeepLOBLite must extract directional content from only 2 levels, making the top-of-book price features individually more influential.

\section{Code and Reproducibility}
The codebase separates heavy computation from presentation:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \texttt{scripts/train\_deeplob.py} trains DeepLOB on FI-2010 under a specified profile (\texttt{--horizon-profile paper} or \texttt{adaptive}). Outputs land in \texttt{results/fi\_paper/} or \texttt{results/fi\_adaptive/} respectively.
    \item \texttt{scripts/analyze\_fi2010.py} runs feature tests, qualified-factor selection, baseline models, and trading-style diagnostics. It reads the FI training outputs and writes analysis artifacts into the same profile directory.
    \item \texttt{scripts/prepare\_optiver.py} preprocesses Optiver data with causal rolling normalization, producing per-stock \texttt{.npz} files under \texttt{data/optiver\_processed/}.
    \item \texttt{scripts/train\_optiver.py} runs universal base training, transfer fine-tuning, and specific-stock OOS evaluation. Set \texttt{OPTIVER\_LABEL\_MODE=rolling-quantile-3class} (default) to reproduce the results reported here; outputs land under \texttt{results/optiver/classification\_rolling3\_w20000/}.
    \item \texttt{scripts/analyze\_optiver.py} produces the transfer figures (figure6 accuracy, figure7 profit t-stats, figure8 cumulative profit, figure9 LIME explanation) and the \texttt{transfer\_analysis\_summary.json} summary read by the Optiver notebook.
    \item \texttt{run\_deeplob\_pytorch.ipynb} is the FI-2010 report notebook. It loads from \texttt{results/fi\_paper/} (primary for $k{=}1$--$3$) and \texttt{results/fi\_adaptive/} (primary for $k{=}4$--$5$) and presents both profiles side by side for comparison.
    \item \texttt{run\_optiver.ipynb} is the Optiver report notebook. It loads from \texttt{results/optiver/classification\_rolling3\_w20000/} and presents base diagnostics, transfer comparison, and cumulative-profit figures.

Expensive training stages (SLURM GPU jobs) are decoupled from the notebooks so the report surface remains clean and fast to render. The submission includes all saved model checkpoints (\texttt{models/}), prediction arrays (\texttt{results/*/preds\_*.npz}), and metric pickles (\texttt{results/*/\*\_metrics\_*.pkl}) so every quantitative claim in this report can be regenerated from the stored artifacts without re-running GPU training.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
Our project has several limitations. On the FI-2010 side, the benchmark is highly standardized and may not reflect heterogeneous real-market conditions. The paper profile achieves strong short-horizon accuracy, but this advantage could partially reflect task-specific overfitting to the FI-2010 normalization scheme rather than generalizable short-horizon understanding. The pseudo-PnL diagnostics are intentionally simple and omit execution frictions, so they serve only as a consistency check rather than a trading claim.

On the Optiver side, our study is narrow by design: three held-out stocks, a single horizon, and a fixed architecture. The rolling-quantile labeling scheme (\texttt{rolling3}) is effective but not exhaustively compared---additional threshold designs (e.g., fixed-return, asymmetric, or volatility-anchored bins) could further clarify the role of label construction. The specific-stock OOS evaluation consistently underperforms zero-shot transfer, but we have not fully isolated whether the dominant cause is temporal drift, insufficient training data, or label-boundary instability within a stock.

Future directions include: (1) a systematic ablation over Optiver label threshold designs and window lengths; (2) replacing the LSTM with a temporal convolutional or attention-based aggregate better suited to shallow two-level books; (3) extending the held-out stock set to test generality of the fine-tuning benefit; and (4) exploring calibration and reject-option approaches that abstain from predicting near the decision boundary.

\section{Conclusion}
This project successfully reproduced DeepLOB on FI-2010 under two training profiles and showed that the choice of checkpointing metric is a first-order determinant of short-horizon performance. The paper-style optimizer with validation-accuracy monitoring achieves the strongest short-horizon benchmark result (accuracy $0.835$, MCC $0.608$ at 10 events), while the adaptive profile's horizon-aware regularization marginally improves agreement metrics at longer horizons. Both profiles substantially outperform shallow manual-factor baselines, and the factor analysis identifies \texttt{u6} (price \& volume derivatives) as the consistently most significant signal group.

On Optiver, the primary finding is that \textbf{per-stock fine-tuning under rolling-quantile labels (\texttt{rolling3}) is the most effective transfer strategy}. The universal model achieves mean $\kappa{=}0.062$ across three held-out stocks; per-stock fine-tuning lifts this to $\kappa{=}0.084$ by recalibrating decision boundaries to each stock's local return distribution. Specific-stock temporal training regresses to $\kappa{=}0.002$, confirming that within-stock nonstationarity is a genuine and hard bottleneck that per-stock fine-tuning partially sidesteps.

The combined picture is: DeepLOB reproduces reliably on FI-2010 (best results: paper profile at short horizons, adaptive at long), and rolling-quantile label adaptation with per-stock fine-tuning is the most effective Optiver transfer strategy identified, but the domain gap between benchmark LOB data and live equity auction books remains large and motivates continued work on both the modeling and the data-representation sides.
